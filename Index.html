Research Argument
The Central Claim
"Michigan Daily concert reviews of minority artists at Hill Auditorium between 1980 and 2000 reflect both the aspirations and the limitations of the Michigan Mandate era — simultaneously celebrating artistic excellence and revealing persistent patterns of exoticization, cultural validation through Western frameworks, and institutional anxiety."

The reviews are not merely entertainment journalism. They are micro-level evidence in a macro-level argument about race, institutional power, and what it means to be a "multicultural university." Each article choice — which instruments to explain, which Western comparisons to reach for, how to justify a genre's presence at Hill — encodes the ideological work the university needed cultural programming to do during the Michigan Mandate era.

About This Project
Nilay Mishra

A digital research exhibit for AMCULT 103, University of Michigan. Examining how campus media framed guest artists from minority racial and ethnic backgrounds at Hill Auditorium between 1980 and 2000, and what this reveals about U-M's multicultural project.

Key Secondary Sources
Full analysis in Sources section →
Matthew Johnson
Undermining Racial Justice (2020)

Johnson argues that racial inclusion can become a strategy for institutional stability rather than genuine justice. His concept of "selective incorporation" — celebrating diversity while preserving structural hierarchies — is the critical framework animating this project's analysis of Michigan Daily language.

James Duderstadt
A 50-Year History of Social Diversity at U-M

The Michigan Mandate's architect provides the institutional narrative of diversification as simultaneous moral imperative and academic strategy. His language of "complementary goals" and necessary "sacrifices" illuminates the ideological framework within which the Michigan Daily reviews were written.

James Tobin
"Michigan's Affirmative Action Debate"

Tobin traces the legal and political pressures that made Michigan's diversity initiatives matters of survival, not merely aspiration. His account of the BAM protests through the Supreme Court cases reveals how high the stakes were when student writers chose how to frame a Ravi Shankar concert or a Run-D.M.C. preview.

Research Approach
"This project moves from the macro-level arguments of institutional history toward the micro-level evidence of individual concert reviews — using language analysis, framing theory, and critical race scholarship to ask what the smallest editorial choices in campus media reveal about multiculturalism's limits and possibilities."
