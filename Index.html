Background

The Stage & The Street

Between 1980 and 2000, the University of Michigan was two institutions at once. Inside Hill Auditorium, world-class artists performed to packed houses of 3,500. Outside—on the Diag, in Fleming Admin Building, at the Michigan Union—students demanded that the university finally look like the world it claimed to serve. These two stages were never separate.

The Venue
Hill Auditorium: "The Other Big House"

Designed by Albert Kahn and opened in 1913, Hill Auditorium was engineered for acoustic perfection — legend holds that a pin dropped on stage could be heard from the upper balcony. By the 1980s, the University Musical Society (UMS) was booking artists of international repute: Yo-Yo Ma, Wynton Marsalis, Ravi Shankar, and Elton John all graced its oval stage.

"Hill Auditorium speaks to the quality and the standards that we expect in our performances."
— Kenneth Kiesler, Director of University Orchestras
The Context
A Campus Demanding Change

In January 1987, racist flyers appeared under dorm-room doors. A student radio show broadcast racist jokes to campus. Black enrollment, which had hit a high of 7.7% in 1977, had slid back to 5.4%. The United Coalition Against Racism (UCAR) and Black Action Movement III mobilized. The protests that followed would reshape the university's identity for a generation.

"The single most important thing we have agreed on is that it is in the interest of the university to increase the representation of Blacks."
— President Harold Shapiro, Hill Auditorium rally, March 1987
The Movement
BAM I, II & III: Three Campaigns

The Black Action Movement ran three distinct campaigns across 1970, 1975, and 1987 — each demanding meaningful Black enrollment and faculty representation. BAM III's 1987 protest, supported by students from across demographics, ultimately led President James Duderstadt to launch the Michigan Mandate: a sweeping institutional plan to make U-M a multicultural university.

"Its thesis — that cultural diversity and education were not antithetical but intimately linked — was an early forerunner of ideas soon taken for granted in higher education."
— Michigan Today, 2024
The Intersection
The Stage as Argument

The UMS programming of this era was itself an implicit argument. Booking Wynton Marsalis (whose JLCO debuted at UMS in 1994), Ravi Shankar (first presented in April 1996), and Sweet Honey in the Rock alongside Vienna Philharmonic and Berlin Philharmonic wasn't just artistic curation — it was a statement about what "world-class" could mean. The artists on stage and the protesters in the streets were both insisting on a more expansive America.

"The spirit here…you can get the type of student participation you get, and bring the type of people that you've brought, the quality of the orchestras — everything is on the highest level."
— Wynton Marsalis, on Hill Auditorium

Chronology

Twenty Years,
One Stage

A parallel record of landmark performances at Hill Auditorium and the campus activism reshaping the university during the same years.

1980
Campus
Reagan Era Begins — Conservative Resurgence on Campus

As Ronald Reagan wins the presidency, Black student enrollment at U-M continues a decline from its 1977 high of 7.7%. The national conservative shift pressures affirmative action programs across higher education. UMS continues its mission of bringing world-class performers to Hill, including the Vienna Philharmonic and Berlin Philharmonic.

1982
Music
Yo-Yo Ma Performs at Hill Auditorium

The young cellist — already a prodigy who had debuted with the Harvard-Radcliffe Orchestra at age 15 — performs with UMS. His appearances at Hill across the 1980s and 90s become touchstones for U-M audiences. Ma would later join U-M for a formal residency spanning all three campuses. "Life, like music, is based on themes and variations," he told a Hill Auditorium audience.

1984
Campus
Black Enrollment Drops to 5.1%

After hovering near 7% for several years, Black enrollment at U-M falls to 5.1% — far short of the 10% target first demanded by BAM I in 1970. Declining recruitment, reduced financial aid, and a hostile campus climate all contribute. Student organizations begin organizing for a third major campaign.

1987
Activism
BAM III & UCAR: The Third Wave of Protest

In January, racist flyers appear in dorms. In February, the student radio station WJJX broadcasts racist jokes. The United Coalition Against Racism (UCAR) forms as a multiracial coalition. In March, Black Action Movement III emerges, demanding $35,000 for the BSU, tenure for Black faculty, and a $5 million recruitment plan. Rev. Jesse Jackson mediates. On March 24, a standing-room-only rally of 4,200 fills Hill Auditorium — where President Shapiro announces an agreement.

1988
Policy
Michigan Mandate Launched Under President Duderstadt

Newly installed President James Duderstadt launches the Michigan Mandate — a sweeping institutional commitment to build a genuinely multicultural university. Its founding thesis: that cultural diversity and education are not in tension but are deeply linked. The plan funds minority recruitment, supports the Ella Baker–Nelson Mandela Freedom Center, and repositions diversity as a core institutional value.

1989
Music
Grateful Dead at Crisler Arena — Ann Arbor Embraces the Dead

The Grateful Dead play two sold-out nights at Crisler Arena, with over 12,000 fans each night. Michigan Daily writer Brian Jarvinen previews the shows, highlighting the Dead's ever-shifting setlists. The Ann Arbor News describes a "classically rambling show of over three hours" rich in musical value. Hill Auditorium had famously hosted the band in 1971 for legendary two-night radio broadcasts.

1993
Policy
Office of Minority Affairs Becomes OAMI

Dr. Lester Monts becomes Vice Provost for Academic and Multicultural Affairs. The Office of Minority Affairs is renamed the Office of Academic Multicultural Initiatives (OAMI), reflecting the broadened mission of the Michigan Mandate era. OAMI becomes a hub for recruiting, retaining, and supporting students of color across U-M.

1994
Music
Wynton Marsalis & Jazz at Lincoln Center Orchestra Debut at UMS

The Jazz at Lincoln Center Orchestra makes its UMS debut — the beginning of a relationship that would see Marsalis return over 17 times. Committed since 1988 to honoring the lineage of Duke Ellington and Louis Armstrong while performing new works, JLCO brings jazz as both art and civic practice to Hill Auditorium. "Education is the future of any idiom," JLCO drummer Ali Jackson said. Marsalis would go on to receive U-M's honorary Doctor of Music at the 2023 commencement.

1995
Arts
Last UMS May Festival — An Era Ends

The May Festival, a cornerstone of UMS programming since its founding in 1879, holds its final edition. The festival had been central to Ann Arbor's cultural identity for over a century. The UMS Distinguished Artist Award is inaugurated in May 1996 to replace it, recognizing artists with lasting educational contributions — JLCO would receive it in 2014.

1996
Music
Ravi Shankar First Presented at UMS — Rackham Auditorium

Legendary sitarist Ravi Shankar makes his first UMS appearance at Rackham Auditorium in April 1996. The New York Times called him "the sitarist who introduced Indian music to the West." His appearances in Ann Arbor span decades, with a later solo performance at Hill Auditorium in September 2004. A UMS essayist wrote: "He never lost sight of the tradition from which he came. This is the true nature of innovation."

1996
Campus
Black Enrollment Reaches 9% — Michigan Mandate's High Watermark

Black enrollment at U-M reaches 9% — the highest in the university's history and the closest the campus ever came to the 10% benchmark first demanded by BAM I in 1970. The Ella Baker–Nelson Mandela Freedom Center, which had been pivotal in political education and campus organizing since 1988, closes this same year after nearly a decade of activism.

1999
Music
Wynton Marsalis Premieres All Rise — Commissioned for the Millennium

Marsalis's massive jazz symphony All Rise (Symphony No. 1) is commissioned by the New York Philharmonic and has its world premiere in December 1999. The 12-movement work blends African chant, New Orleans parade music, gospel, and symphonic modernism. Marsalis described it as "the rise from destruction to creativity, drawing joy out of tragedy." UMS would later present it at Hill Auditorium with the U-M Symphony Orchestra and Choirs.

2000
Campus
BSU & Students of Color Coalition Occupy Michigan Union Tower

The Black Student Union joins the Students of Color Coalition in a 37-day sit-in at the Michigan Union tower, protesting the Michigamua secret society's use of offensive depictions of Native Americans in its rituals. The action marks the close of the era documented in this project — the BAM legacy continues to shape activism at U-M into the 21st century.

Performers

Artists of
the Era

The performers who graced Hill Auditorium's oval stage between 1980 and 2000 — many of them appearing through the University Musical Society — were not merely entertainment. Their presence in Ann Arbor was inseparable from the cultural moment happening outside the auditorium's doors.

UMS Debut 1994 · 17+ Visits
Wynton Marsalis
Jazz Trumpet · Composer · Educator

"The spirit here — the size of Hill, the student participation, the quality of the orchestras — everything is on the highest level."

Born in New Orleans in 1961, Marsalis joined Art Blakey's Jazz Messengers in 1980 and assembled his own band in 1981. He rekindled jazz's mainstream appeal through the 1980s and 90s, touring over 120 concerts per year for 15 consecutive years. His Jazz at Lincoln Center Orchestra debuted at UMS in 1994 and became nearly annual fixtures at Hill Auditorium. Marsalis received U-M's honorary Doctor of Music at the 2023 commencement.

Yo-Yo Ma
Classical Cello · Cultural Ambassador

"Life, like music, is based on themes and variations, experiences and experiments that shape who we are."

Born in Paris to Chinese parents, Yo-Yo Ma debuted with the Harvard-Radcliffe Orchestra at age 15 and became one of the defining musicians of his generation. His appearances at Hill Auditorium spanned decades. He later joined U-M for a formal residency across all three campuses. "Life, like music, is based on themes and variations," he told a packed Hill Auditorium audience — a philosophy that resonated during the university's years of transformation.

Ravi Shankar
Hindustani Sitar · Composer · Teacher

"He never lost sight of the tradition from which he came. This is the true nature of innovation." — UMS essayist

India's most esteemed musical ambassador and "the sitarist who introduced Indian music to the West" (New York Times). A prolific composer of ragas and talas, he collaborated with George Harrison, Philip Glass, and Yehudi Menuhin. UMS first presented him in April 1996 at Rackham Auditorium. Even at 85, he was still listening to and evaluating his own music.

Hill Auditorium · Ann Arbor
Elton John
Rock · Pop · Piano

One of the first rock superstars to play Hill Auditorium — his appearances expanded the venue's identity beyond classical music.

Among the most commercially successful artists of the rock era, Elton John performed at Hill Auditorium as part of his extensive North American touring throughout the 1980s. His appearances at the hall placed pop and rock squarely alongside the UMS classical and jazz programming — a broadening of what Hill Auditorium could mean to Ann Arbor audiences. Wikipedia and Michigan Today both cite John's Hill performances among the venue's landmark pop moments.

Grateful Dead
Psychedelic Rock · Live Performance

Their 1971 Hill Auditorium radio broadcasts are among the most celebrated live recordings in rock history.

The Grateful Dead played Hill Auditorium for legendary two-night radio broadcasts in December 1971 — one of the most documented runs in the band's history. By the 1980s and 90s they moved to the larger Crisler Arena, drawing 12,000+ fans for their 1989 Ann Arbor run. A Michigan Daily writer previewed the 1989 shows, noting the band's ever-shifting setlists. Their 1971 Hill appearances remain among the most celebrated recordings of the Dead's early catalog.

Sweet Honey in the Rock
A Cappella · African American Tradition · Social Justice

Founded by Dr. Bernice Johnson Reagon — their UMS performances embodied the era's push to expand whose voices Hill amplified.

Founded in 1973 by Dr. Bernice Johnson Reagon, Sweet Honey in the Rock is an a cappella ensemble rooted in African American musical traditions — gospel, blues, jazz, spirituals, and R&B. Their performances at UMS, documented in the UMS digital archive, were emblematic of the era's commitment to expanding whose voices Hill Auditorium would amplify. Their work bridged the worlds of artistic excellence and social justice advocacy.

Activism

BAM & The Fight
For A Multicultural U-M

I
1970 — BAM I
Strike That Changed the University

Black students walk out of classes for 13 days. Over 300 professors cancel classes. The university agrees to a 10% Black enrollment target by 1973-74. Key demands: a Black student center (today's Trotter Multicultural Center), expanded Black studies, and new funding for minority recruitment. Protesters disrupted the annual honors convocation with tear gas bombs. BAM picketers lined the steps of Hill Auditorium at the March convocation.

II
1975 — BAM II
Confronting Broken Promises

Black enrollment stood at only 7.3% — the university had not reached its own stated goal. BAM II protesters occupied the Administration Building. President Fleming negotiated directly with students. The protest exposed the gap between institutional promises and institutional follow-through — a pattern that would define the decades to come.

Bentley Library → BAM II Documents
III
1987 — BAM III & UCAR
Two Groups, One Demand: Change

Sparked by racist flyers in dorms and a racist radio broadcast on WJJX, both BAM III and the multiracial United Coalition Against Racism mobilize. Rev. Jesse Jackson mediates. Six concessions are reached, including creation of the Office of Minority Affairs. The 4,200-person rally at Hill Auditorium becomes the symbolic moment where music venue and activist space literally converge.

1988–1996 — The Mandate Era
The Michigan Mandate: Institutionalizing Change

President Duderstadt's Michigan Mandate transforms the university's diversity approach. Black enrollment rises from 5.4% in 1987 to a record 9% by 1996. The Ella Baker–Nelson Mandela Freedom Center engages in political education across campus. The Office of Minority Affairs — renamed OAMI under Dr. Lester Monts in 1993 — becomes a permanent institutional pillar.

1988
The Michigan Mandate: A New Kind of University

Launched in 1988 under President James Duderstadt, the Michigan Mandate represented the most ambitious institutional response to student activism in U-M's history. Its foundational argument — that diversity strengthens rather than dilutes educational excellence — was a direct answer to the conservative backlash of the Reagan era. By framing multiculturalism as an academic imperative, Duderstadt aligned the university's scholarly mission with the demands students had been making for nearly two decades. The arts played a subtle but real role: UMS programming from 1988 onward reflected an expanding definition of world-class performance — one that included jazz, Indian classical music, African American vocal tradition, and global dance forms alongside the European canon.

The Stage
Never Stood
Alone

Between 1980 and 2000, the University of Michigan lived a double life. In Hill Auditorium, Yo-Yo Ma drew his bow across strings in a hall designed for acoustical perfection. On the Diag, students demanded that the university make good on promises two decades old. These were not separate stories. The same institution that celebrated world-class artistic achievement was also the institution that had let Black enrollment slide to 5%. The Michigan Mandate's core insight — that diversity and excellence reinforce each other — was, in some ways, what the programming at Hill Auditorium had been quietly arguing all along.

By 2000, the transformation was incomplete and contested — it remains so. But the 20 years documented here represent a moment when a great university was forced to reckon with the gap between its aspirations and its practices. The stages — physical and metaphorical — were never neutral. They were always sites of argument, memory, and possibility.
